## 1. Executive Summary

The Trotter-Variational Hybrid (TVH) is a parameterised quantum circuit
discovered through data-driven analysis of ZX motif fingerprints. It was
constructed to fill a structural gap identified in the phylogeny of 32
registered quantum algorithms, exploiting the finding that QAOA MaxCut
(variational family) and Trotter Ising simulation (simulation family) share
up to 93.6% motif similarity at the ZX-calculus level.

TVH's central design principle is **structural hybridisation**: it combines
the ZZ-interaction backbone shared by QAOA and Trotter with two additional
motifs (`cluster_chain` and `hadamard_sandwich`) that survive full ZX
reduction. This creates a circuit that bridges three algorithmic paradigms:
digital Hamiltonian simulation, variational optimisation, and
measurement-based quantum computation.

The circuit has two continuous parameters (gamma for ZZ coupling strength,
beta for mixer/basis-change angle), uses 60 gates at depth 31 for 4 qubits
with 2 layers, and scales linearly in all circuit metrics. Its ZX diagram
achieves 69% vertex reduction under full simplification, the highest among
the three discovery candidates, indicating a large Clifford component with
a compact non-Clifford core.

Among the three candidate algorithms generated by the discovery pipeline,
TVH achieved the lowest novelty score (0.257), reflecting its successful
bridging of two already-similar families. Its nearest existing algorithm is
`cluster_state_q3` from the entanglement family (cosine similarity 0.743),
an unexpected affinity that reveals the dominance of the cluster chain motif
in TVH's structural fingerprint.

## 2. Discovery Context

### 2.1 The Phylogeny Pipeline

The discovery of TVH followed a two-stage pipeline built on this repository's
ZX motif analysis tools:

**Stage 1** (`scripts/discover_phylogeny.py`): Fingerprinted 88 instances of
32 quantum algorithms across 10 families by counting occurrences of 367 ZX
motifs at the spider-fused simplification level. Hierarchical clustering and
PCA revealed the structural relationships between algorithm families.

**Stage 2** (`scripts/discover_algorithm.py`): Used the phylogeny findings to
identify structural gaps and construct candidate circuits to fill them.

### 2.2 The Motivating Finding

The phylogeny analysis produced a ranked list of cross-family structural
relatives. The second most striking cluster (after the BBPSSW-GHZ pairing)
was between the variational and simulation families:

| Algorithm A | Family A | Algorithm B | Family B | Cosine Similarity |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| qaoa_maxcut_q6 | variational | trotter_ising_q6 | simulation | 0.936 |
| qaoa_maxcut_q5 | variational | trotter_ising_q6 | simulation | 0.933 |
| qaoa_maxcut_q6 | variational | trotter_ising_q5 | simulation | 0.931 |
| qaoa_maxcut_q5 | variational | trotter_ising_q5 | simulation | 0.929 |
| qaoa_maxcut_q4 | variational | trotter_ising_q6 | simulation | 0.926 |
| qaoa_maxcut_q4 | variational | trotter_ising_q5 | simulation | 0.924 |
| qaoa_maxcut_q3 | variational | trotter_ising_q5 | simulation | 0.909 |
| qaoa_maxcut_q3 | variational | trotter_ising_q6 | simulation | 0.906 |

QAOA MaxCut and Trotter Ising simulation, which solve entirely different
problems (combinatorial optimisation vs. Hamiltonian time evolution), share
over 93% ZX motif similarity. Both families build on the same structural
core: the ZZ-interaction motif (CX-RZ-CX) that implements
exp(-i * angle * Z_i * Z_{i+1}).

The structural similarity is grounded in physics: both algorithms encode
pairwise ZZ interactions between neighbouring qubits, differing only in the
context and purpose of those interactions (variational optimisation of a
classical cost function vs. approximation of continuous time evolution).

### 2.3 The Coverage Gap

The motif coverage analysis revealed that:
- The **variational** family had 73.6% motif coverage (below the corpus mean of 81.8%)
- The **simulation** family had 86.2% motif coverage (above average)

The variational family's below-average coverage indicated that its structural
space was not fully captured by the existing motif library, leaving room for
novel structural combinations.

### 2.4 Design Rationale

TVH was constructed to bridge the structural gap between the variational and
simulation families by:

1. Starting from the shared structural core (ZZ-interaction backbone via CX-RZ-CX)
2. Adding `cluster_chain`, a motif that survives full ZX reduction and appears in entanglement, error correction, and variational families
3. Adding `hadamard_sandwich`, a motif specific to the simulation family that also survives full ZX reduction
4. Incorporating a QAOA-style RX mixer layer for variational driving

The explicit inclusion of ZX-reduction-surviving motifs was a deliberate
design choice: these motifs represent structures that persist through all
levels of ZX simplification, forming the "irreducible skeleton" of the
circuit. By incorporating them alongside the Trotter backbone, TVH was
designed to occupy a region of ZX motif space that neither QAOA nor Trotter
alone reaches.

## 3. Circuit Definition

### 3.1 Generator Function

```python
def make_trotter_variational_hybrid(
    n_qubits: int = 4,
    n_layers: int = 2,
    gamma: float = 0.7,
    beta: float = 0.4,
) -> QuantumCircuit:
```

**Parameters:**
- `n_qubits`: Total number of qubits (minimum 3). All qubits participate
  in all stages.
- `n_layers`: Number of repeated layers (default 2). Each layer applies the
  full four-stage sequence.
- `gamma`: ZZ coupling angle in radians. Controls the strength of Ising-like
  interactions. Default 0.7.
- `beta`: Mixer and basis-change angle in radians. Controls the strength of
  the transverse field and hadamard sandwich rotations. Default 0.4.

### 3.2 Circuit Construction

Each of the `n_layers` layers applies four stages:

```
Stage 1 -- ZZ interaction backbone (Trotter structure):
    For i in 0..n-2:
        CNOT(q_i, q_{i+1})
        RZ(gamma, q_{i+1})
        CNOT(q_i, q_{i+1})

Stage 2 -- Cluster chain entangling layer:
    H(q_i)         for i in 0..n-1
    CZ(q_i, q_{i+1})  for i in 0..n-2
    H(q_i)         for i in 0..n-1

Stage 3 -- Hadamard sandwich basis-change layer:
    For i in 0, 2, 4, ..., n-2:
        H(q_i)
        RZ(beta, q_i)
        H(q_i)

Stage 4 -- QAOA-style mixer rotations:
    RX(2*beta, q_i)   for i in 0..n-1
```

### 3.4 Functional Decomposition

The four stages serve distinct purposes drawn from different algorithmic
traditions:

| Stage | Gates per Layer (n=4) | Origin | Purpose |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1. ZZ backbone | 9 (3 RZ + 6 CX) | Trotter / QAOA | Nearest-neighbour ZZ interactions. Shared structural core between simulation and variational families. |
| 2. Cluster chain | 11 (8 H + 3 CZ) | Graph states / MBQC | Graph-state entanglement. Survives full ZX reduction. Adds entanglement structure absent from both parent families. |
| 3. Hadamard sandwich | 6 (4 H + 2 RZ) | Simulation (specific) | Basis-change rotations. Equivalent to RX(beta) rotations. Survives full ZX reduction. |
| 4. Mixer | 4 (4 RX) | QAOA | Transverse-field driving term. Provides variational control over the mixing angle. |

### 3.5 Physical Interpretation

The ZZ backbone (Stage 1) implements a single Trotter step of the
nearest-neighbour Ising Hamiltonian:

```
H_ZZ = -gamma * SUM_{i} Z_i * Z_{i+1}
```

Each CX-RZ(gamma)-CX triplet realizes exp(-i * gamma * Z_i * Z_{i+1}).
This is the structural element shared at 93% similarity between QAOA
MaxCut and Trotter Ising simulation.

The cluster chain (Stage 2) creates a 1D cluster state in the Hadamard
basis. The pattern H-CZ-H on a chain is equivalent (up to local phases)
to creating a graph state, the universal resource for measurement-based
quantum computation. This layer adds entanglement correlations that go
beyond what the ZZ backbone alone produces.

The hadamard sandwich (Stage 3) applies H-RZ(beta)-H = RX(beta) rotations
on even-indexed qubits, creating coherent rotations in the X basis. Combined
with the cluster chain, this embeds a controlled-phase structure that
survives ZX simplification.

The mixer (Stage 4) applies RX(2*beta) on all qubits, serving the same
role as QAOA's mixer unitary: exp(-i * beta * X_i) drives transitions
between computational basis states.

## 5. Mathematical Properties

### 5.1 Unitarity

TVH implements a valid unitary transformation. Verified via Qiskit's
`Operator` class:

```
U * U^dagger = I   (error: 1.06e-15, machine precision)
```

### 5.2 Complex Unitary

Unlike DEB (which is entirely real-valued), the TVH unitary matrix contains
complex entries. This follows from the gate set: while H, CX, and CZ are
real in the standard representation, the RZ and RX gates have complex matrix
elements:

```
RZ(theta) = diag(e^{-i*theta/2}, e^{+i*theta/2})
RX(theta) = [[cos(theta/2), -i*sin(theta/2)],
             [-i*sin(theta/2), cos(theta/2)]]
```

Consequence: TVH can explore complex-valued regions of Hilbert space that
a purely real ansatz (like DEB) cannot reach. This is relevant for
simulating Hamiltonians with complex off-diagonal elements (e.g.,
Heisenberg models with external magnetic fields).

### 5.3 Gate Set Universality

TVH uses the gate set {H, CX, CZ, RZ, RX}. Since {H, CX, RZ} is already
a universal gate set for quantum computation, TVH's gate set is universal.
The CZ gates are Clifford and could be decomposed into H + CX + H, but are
retained as native gates because they create the cluster chain motif that
drives TVH's structural identity.

### 5.4 Layer Structure and Expressibility

With L layers, TVH applies the parameterised unitary:

```
U(gamma, beta) = [U_mix(beta) * U_hs(beta) * U_cc * U_zz(gamma)]^L
```

where:
- U_zz(gamma) = Product of exp(-i * gamma * Z_i * Z_{i+1})
- U_cc = cluster chain unitary (fixed, no free parameters)
- U_hs(beta) = Product of RX(beta) on even qubits
- U_mix(beta) = Product of RX(2*beta) on all qubits

The cluster chain U_cc is parameter-free: it always applies the same
graph-state entanglement pattern. This means the entanglement structure
created by the cluster chain is invariant under parameter changes. Only
the ZZ coupling (gamma) and mixer strength (beta) are tuneable.

This separation of tuneable and fixed entanglement layers is a distinctive
structural feature: the cluster chain provides a "backbone" of entanglement
that the variational parameters modulate but do not create or destroy.

### 5.5 Tensor Preservation Under ZX Simplification

```
zx.compare_tensors(raw_graph, fully_reduced_graph) = True
```

The fully reduced ZX graph (27 vertices, 55 edges) implements the same
unitary as the original circuit (86 vertices, 100 edges). The 69% vertex
reduction removes all Clifford-redundant structure while preserving
computational content exactly.

## 6. The Gamma and Beta Parameters

### 6.1 Role of Gamma (ZZ Coupling)

Gamma controls the strength of nearest-neighbour ZZ interactions in the
Trotter backbone. Each CX-RZ(gamma)-CX block implements:

```
exp(-i * gamma * Z_i * Z_{i+1})
```

| Regime | Gamma | Character |
|---|---|---|
| Weak coupling | 0 | No ZZ interaction. Circuit reduces to cluster chain + rotations. |
| Standard operating point | 0.7 | Moderate Ising coupling. Default for analysis. |
| Trotter pi/4 point | pi/4 | T-gate-like phase. Creates non-Clifford entanglement. |
| Strong coupling | pi/2 | Maximal ZZ interaction. Equivalent to a CZ gate on each pair. |
| Full rotation | pi | Clifford point (Z gate). ZZ interaction is purely Clifford. |

At gamma = 0, the ZZ backbone becomes trivial (CX * I * CX = I), and TVH
reduces to just the cluster chain, hadamard sandwich, and mixer layers.

At gamma = pi, the RZ(pi) = Z gate makes the ZZ backbone entirely Clifford,
and it would fuse into the cluster chain under ZX simplification.

The default gamma = 0.7 places the circuit in a non-Clifford regime where
the ZZ backbone creates genuine quantum correlations that cannot be
classically simulated.

### 6.2 Role of Beta (Mixer/Basis-Change)

Beta controls two things simultaneously:
1. The RX rotation in the hadamard sandwich (Stage 3): H-RZ(beta)-H = RX(beta)
2. The mixer strength (Stage 4): RX(2*beta)

This coupling means beta controls the transverse-field strength: larger
beta drives stronger transitions between computational basis states.

| Regime | Beta | Character |
|---|---|---|
| No mixing | 0 | No X-rotations. Circuit creates entanglement without variational mixing. |
| Standard operating point | 0.4 | Moderate mixing. Default for analysis. |
| Balanced | pi/4 | Equal ZZ and X-field strengths (at default gamma=0.7). |
| Strong mixing | pi/2 | Strong transverse field. Mixer dominates. |

### 6.3 Parameter Space

TVH has a 2-dimensional parameter space (gamma, beta), compared to DEB's
1-dimensional space (theta) and ICC's 0-dimensional space (no free
parameters). With L layers, independent per-layer parameters would give
a 2L-dimensional space, though the default configuration ties all layers
to the same (gamma, beta).

The 2D parameter space enables TVH to interpolate between qualitatively
different physical regimes:

```
                      beta
                       ^
                       |
     ZZ + strong mix   |   balanced hybrid
     (QAOA-like)       |   (default: 0.7, 0.4)
                       |
      -----------------+----------------> gamma
                       |
     no interactions   |   pure ZZ coupling
     (trivial)         |   (Trotter-like)
                       |
```

The default operating point (gamma=0.7, beta=0.4) places TVH in the
upper-right quadrant where both ZZ coupling and mixing are active,
combining Trotter and QAOA characteristics.

## 7. Three Entanglement Mechanisms

### 7.1 Overview

TVH is unique among the discovery candidates in employing three distinct
entanglement mechanisms per layer. Each mechanism creates a different type
of quantum correlation:

| Mechanism | Stage | Gate Pattern | Entanglement Type | Origin |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| ZZ interaction | 1 | CX-RZ-CX | Ising-like ZZ correlations | Trotter / QAOA |
| Cluster chain | 2 | H-CZ-H chain | Graph-state (stabiliser) | MBQC |
| Mixer | 4 | RX rotations | Coherent superposition | QAOA |

### 7.2 ZZ Interaction (Stage 1)

The CX-RZ(gamma)-CX pattern creates nearest-neighbour correlations of the
form:

```
|psi> -> cos(gamma/2)|psi> - i*sin(gamma/2) * (Z_i * Z_{i+1})|psi>
```

This generates entanglement only between adjacent qubit pairs. The
entanglement is parameterised by gamma and vanishes at gamma = 0. At
gamma = pi/4, the generated correlations are maximally non-Clifford.

### 7.3 Cluster Chain (Stage 2)

The H-CZ-H chain creates a 1D cluster state. In the ZX calculus, CZ gates
between Hadamard-conjugated qubits create a chain of Z-spiders connected by
Hadamard edges: the `cluster_chain` motif.

The cluster chain is parameter-free and always creates the same entanglement
pattern. This provides a fixed backbone of graph-state entanglement that
persists through ZX simplification. The cluster state structure is:

```
|cluster> = Product_i CZ(i, i+1) * H^{tensor n} |0...0>
```

In the ZX representation, this appears as a linear chain of Z-spiders
connected by Hadamard (blue) edges, which is the canonical form of a 1D
cluster state.

### 7.4 Mixer (Stage 4)

The RX(2*beta) rotations on all qubits create coherent superpositions in
the computational basis. While each RX gate acts locally (single-qubit),
when applied after the entangling layers, they generate correlations between
measurement outcomes that serve as the "driving term" in QAOA.

### 7.5 Interplay Between Mechanisms

The three mechanisms interact constructively:

1. **ZZ + cluster chain**: The ZZ backbone creates diagonal (phase) correlations, while the cluster chain creates off-diagonal (amplitude) correlations. Together, they generate a richer entanglement structure than either alone.

2. **Cluster chain + mixer**: The cluster state provides a structured entanglement backbone that the mixer rotations can explore. This is analogous to how MBQC uses measurement angles to steer computation through a cluster state.

3. **Layer repetition**: Repeating the four-stage sequence creates deeper entanglement. The second layer's ZZ interactions act on states already entangled by the first layer's cluster chain, creating longer-range correlations.

This three-way interaction is the structural feature that distinguishes TVH
from both QAOA (which has only ZZ + mixer) and Trotter (which has only ZZ
+ transverse field).

## 8. Phylogenetic Placement

### 8.1 Novelty Score

TVH achieved the lowest novelty score (0.257) among the three candidate
algorithms, compared to DEB (0.654) and ICC (0.359). The novelty score is
defined as 1 minus the maximum cosine similarity to any of the 88 existing
algorithm instances.

The nearest existing algorithm is `cluster_state_q3` from the entanglement
family, at a cosine similarity of 0.743. This affinity is driven by TVH's

### 8.2 The Unexpected Entanglement Affinity

TVH was designed to bridge the variational and simulation families, but its
nearest neighbour is from the entanglement family. This reveals a key
insight: **the cluster chain motif dominates TVH's structural fingerprint**.

The ZZ-interaction backbone, despite being the motivating structural
element, becomes relatively inconspicuous at the ZX level because the
CX-RZ-CX pattern simplifies under spider fusion to compact phase gadget
structures. In contrast, the cluster chain's Hadamard-edge chain pattern
remains distinctive and is the largest contributor to TVH's fingerprint.

This means TVH's structural identity is defined more by what was *added*
(cluster chain) than by what was *inherited* (ZZ backbone), an unintended
consequence of the data-driven design approach.

## 9. ZX-Calculus Structure

### 9.1 Raw ZX Graph (4 qubits, 2 layers)

The raw ZX diagram has 86 vertices and 100 edges. The vertex composition
reflects the layered gate structure:

- **Boundary vertices**: 8 (4 inputs + 4 outputs)
- **Z-spiders**: majority, from CX/CZ decomposition, RZ rotation, and Hadamard conjugation
- **X-spiders**: from RX mixer rotations and Hadamard-conjugated operations

The 2-layer structure creates a periodic pattern in the ZX graph, with each
layer contributing approximately 39 non-boundary vertices.

### 9.2 Simplified ZX Graph

After full ZX reduction: 27 vertices, 55 edges (69% vertex reduction).

The reduction is driven by:
1. **Spider fusion**: Adjacent same-colour spiders merge (e.g., consecutive H gates cancel, CZ + H patterns simplify)
2. **Clifford elimination**: Clifford-phase spiders (phases that are multiples of pi/2) are absorbed by the simplification rules
3. **Identity removal**: CX-CX pairs and other self-inverse patterns cancel

The reduced graph retains only the non-Clifford content:
- RZ(gamma) rotations from the ZZ backbone (gamma = 0.7 is non-Clifford)
- RZ(beta) rotations from the hadamard sandwich (beta = 0.4 is non-Clifford)
- RX(2*beta) rotations from the mixer (2*beta = 0.8 is non-Clifford)

### 9.3 High Reduction Ratio

TVH's 69% vertex reduction is notable for two reasons:

1. **More Clifford content than DEB**: DEB achieves only 39% reduction because its RY rotations are non-Clifford and cannot be simplified. TVH's cluster chain (entirely Clifford) and Hadamard layers contribute many simplifiable gates.

2. **Dense reduced core**: The 27-vertex reduced graph has 55 edges, giving an average degree of approximately 4.1 edges per vertex. This is much denser than the raw graph (100/86 = 1.2 edges per vertex), indicating that the non-Clifford core has a highly interconnected structure.

The high connectivity of the reduced graph suggests that TVH's non-Clifford
content is deeply entangled, consistent with the three-mechanism
entanglement structure described in Section 7.

### 9.4 Tensor Preservation

```
zx.compare_tensors(raw_graph, fully_reduced_graph) = True
```

The fully reduced ZX graph implements the same unitary as the original
circuit. This confirms that the ZX simplification rules correctly identify
and remove all redundant structure while preserving the computational
content.

### 9.5 Motif Survival Under Reduction

A distinctive feature of TVH's design is the inclusion of two motifs that
survive full ZX reduction:

| Motif | Survival | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| cluster_chain | Survives | H-edge chains between Z-spiders cannot be further simplified when connecting non-Clifford vertices |
| hadamard_sandwich | Survives | Clifford Z-spider between two non-Clifford Z-spiders is preserved as a structural bridge |
| zz_interaction | Partially simplified | CX-RZ-CX fuses to a phase gadget under spider fusion, but the non-Clifford phase is preserved |
| cx_pair | Simplified away | Adjacent CX pairs cancel; isolated CX gates fuse with neighbours |

The survival of `cluster_chain` and `hadamard_sandwich` in the reduced graph
is by design: these motifs were selected precisely because they persist
through simplification, forming TVH's structural skeleton.

## 10. Applications

### 10.1 Hamiltonian Simulation

**Scenario**: Approximating time evolution under a nearest-neighbour Ising
Hamiltonian with transverse field.

**Protocol**:
1. Initialise |0...0>.
2. Apply TVH with gamma = J*dt (coupling strength times time step) and beta proportional to the transverse field strength.
3. Measure in the computational basis.

**Interpretation**: The ZZ backbone implements the first-order Trotter
decomposition of the ZZ interaction terms, while the mixer implements the
transverse field. The cluster chain layer adds additional correlations that
improve the approximation for states with cluster-state-like entanglement.

**Advantage over bare Trotter**: The cluster chain layer enriches the
entanglement structure, potentially improving the representation of
correlated ground states at the same circuit depth.

**Limitation**: The fixed cluster chain pattern may introduce systematic
bias for Hamiltonians whose ground states do not have graph-state-like
correlations.

### 10.2 Combinatorial Optimisation (QAOA)

**Scenario**: Solving MaxCut or similar combinatorial optimisation problems
on graphs with nearest-neighbour connectivity.

**Protocol**:
1. Initialise |+...+> (apply H to |0...0>).
2. Apply TVH layers with gamma encoding the problem graph and beta as a variational parameter.
3. Optimise beta (and optionally gamma) to minimise the cost function expectation value.
4. Sample the output distribution to find approximate solutions.

**Interpretation**: TVH extends QAOA by adding the cluster chain as an
additional entangling layer between the problem unitary (ZZ backbone) and
the mixer. This provides a richer variational manifold than standard QAOA
at the same number of variational parameters.

**Advantage over standard QAOA**: The cluster chain adds non-local
correlations that standard QAOA's ZZ + mixer structure cannot generate at
small layer count. This may improve convergence for problems requiring
long-range correlations.

### 10.3 Variational Quantum Eigensolver (VQE)

**Scenario**: Finding the ground state energy of an Ising-like Hamiltonian.

**Protocol**:
1. Use TVH as the parameterised ansatz: U(gamma, beta)|0...0>.
2. Measure <H> = <psi(gamma,beta)|H|psi(gamma,beta)>.
3. Optimise (gamma, beta) classically to minimise <H>.

**Properties**:
- Two variational parameters provide a compact 2D energy landscape.
- The cluster chain ensures that the ansatz can represent states with stabiliser-like correlations.
- The fixed cluster chain structure provides regularisation, preventing the optimizer from exploring physically irrelevant regions of Hilbert space.

**Limitation**: Two parameters may be insufficient for complex Hamiltonians.
TVH is best used as an initialisation layer (warm start) for deeper variational circuits.

### 10.4 Quantum Feature Map

**Scenario**: Encoding classical data into quantum states for quantum machine
learning.

**Protocol**:
1. Encode classical features x_1, x_2 as gamma = f(x_1) and beta = g(x_2).
2. Apply TVH to generate the feature state |phi(x)>.
3. Compute kernel values k(x, x') = |<phi(x)|phi(x')>|^2.

**Advantage**: TVH's three entanglement mechanisms create a rich feature
space. The cluster chain provides entanglement structure that is
independent of the input data, while the ZZ backbone and mixer encode
data-dependent correlations. This separation of fixed and tuneable
structure may improve generalisation in quantum kernel methods.

### 10.5 Quantum Simulation of Mixed Models

**Scenario**: Simulating physical systems that combine Ising interactions
with graph-state resources, such as measurement-based quantum computation
processes or hybrid classical-quantum systems.

**Protocol**:
1. Set gamma to encode the Ising coupling strength.
2. Set beta to encode the measurement angle in the MBQC picture.
3. Apply TVH to prepare the hybrid state.
4. Measure observables of interest.

**Interpretation**: TVH naturally combines the digital simulation paradigm
(Trotter steps) with the measurement-based paradigm (cluster state
resources). This makes it particularly suited for studying the interface
between circuit-based and measurement-based quantum computation.

## 12. Open Questions

### 12.1 Optimal Parameters for Specific Problems

The default parameters (gamma=0.7, beta=0.4) were chosen for structural
analysis, not for any specific computational task. Systematic optimisation of
(gamma, beta) for benchmark problems (e.g., MaxCut on random graphs, Ising
model ground state search) would establish TVH's practical performance
compared to QAOA and Trotter.

### 12.2 Layer-Dependent Parameters

The current implementation ties all layers to the same (gamma, beta). Allowing
per-layer parameters gamma_l, beta_l would create a 2L-dimensional variational
space. Whether this improves expressibility enough to justify the additional
optimisation cost is an open question.

### 12.3 Cluster Chain Variant Topology

The cluster chain creates 1D (linear) graph-state entanglement. Alternative
topologies (2D grid, tree, random graph) could create different entanglement
structures. Replacing the linear CZ chain with a problem-adapted graph
topology could improve performance for specific problem classes.

### 12.4 Depth-Optimised Compilation

TVH's depth of 31 (for 4 qubits, 2 layers) is relatively high compared to
DEB's depth 6. Hardware-aware compilation (gate reordering, CZ-to-CX
conversion, parallel gate scheduling) could reduce the effective depth
without changing the circuit's unitary. Quantifying the achievable depth
reduction for specific hardware topologies is an open question.

### 12.5 Noise Resilience

Unlike DEB, TVH has no built-in syndrome register for error detection.
Its noise resilience depends entirely on the gate count and depth. The
cluster chain layer, being entirely Clifford, is relatively resistant to
coherent errors (which commute with Clifford gates), but its depth
contribution increases vulnerability to incoherent (depolarising) noise.
A systematic noise analysis under realistic hardware noise models is needed.

### 12.6 Barren Plateaus

TVH's compact 2-parameter space suggests resistance to barren plateaus,
but this has not been formally analysed. The cluster chain layer's fixed
entanglement structure may either help (by restricting the state to a
physically relevant subspace) or hinder (by creating symmetries that flatten
the cost landscape) optimisation.

### 12.7 Expressibility Characterisation

How much of Hilbert space can TVH's 2-parameter family of states explore?
Computing the expressibility (uniform coverage of the Hilbert space) and
entangling capability (range of entanglement achievable) for TVH would
clarify its role relative to QAOA, Trotter, and hardware-efficient ansatze.

### 12.8 Why Cluster State Affinity?

TVH was designed to bridge variational and simulation families, but its
nearest neighbour is `cluster_state_q3` from the entanglement family. Does
this unexpected affinity have computational consequences? Specifically, can
TVH be used as a resource for measurement-based quantum computation, and
does the ZZ backbone improve upon bare cluster states for MBQC?

### 13.4 Verify Key Properties

```python
import numpy as np
from qiskit import QuantumCircuit
from qiskit.quantum_info import Operator

def make_tvh(n_qubits=4, n_layers=2, gamma=0.7, beta=0.4):
    n_qubits = max(3, n_qubits)
    qc = QuantumCircuit(n_qubits)
    for _layer in range(n_layers):
        # 1. ZZ interaction backbone
        for i in range(n_qubits - 1):
            qc.cx(i, i + 1)
            qc.rz(gamma, i + 1)
            qc.cx(i, i + 1)
        # 2. Cluster chain
        qc.h(range(n_qubits))
        for i in range(n_qubits - 1):
            qc.cz(i, i + 1)
        qc.h(range(n_qubits))
        # 3. Hadamard sandwich
        for i in range(0, n_qubits - 1, 2):
            qc.h(i)
            qc.rz(beta, i)
            qc.h(i)
        # 4. Mixer
        for i in range(n_qubits):
            qc.rx(2 * beta, i)
    return qc

# Verify unitarity
qc = make_tvh(4, 2, 0.7, 0.4)
op = Operator(qc)
mat = op.data
n = mat.shape[0]
product = mat @ mat.conj().T
err = float(np.linalg.norm(product - np.eye(n)) / n)
assert err < 1e-10, f"Unitarity error {err} too large"
print(f"VERIFIED: Unitary (error: {err:.2e})")
print(f"Circuit: {qc.num_qubits} qubits, {qc.size()} gates, depth {qc.depth()}")

# Verify complex-valued unitary
imag_norm = np.linalg.norm(mat.imag)
assert imag_norm > 0.1, "Expected complex unitary"
print(f"VERIFIED: Complex unitary (imaginary norm: {imag_norm:.3f})")

# Verify gate counts
assert qc.size() == 60, f"Expected 60 gates, got {qc.size()}"
assert qc.depth() == 31, f"Expected depth 31, got {qc.depth()}"
print("VERIFIED: Gate count and depth match expected values")
```
